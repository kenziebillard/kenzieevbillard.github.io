Misinformation spread like wildfire after Paul Pelosi's hammer attack

More than at any other time in human history, new digital platforms have freed creative journalism practices, enabling unique forms of communication and a wider global audience. On the other hand, the spread of false information and misinformation, also known as "fake news," is accelerating and changing how people see daily events. Many information systems have become more polarized and divisive due to the widespread availability of this news, and public confidence in traditional journalism has sharply decreased. An example of a fake news story is when, on October 28, 2022, a man named David Depape broke into Nancy Pelosi's home in San Francisco. Paul, her 82-year-old husband, was the only one present. Paul Pelosi was severely beaten with a hammer by the intruder. The occurrence before long produced a misleading paranoid fear guaranteeing that Paul Pelosi had brought the one who went after him home from a gay bar. Elon Musk amplified the same rumour in a tweet that has since been deleted, just a few days after he reportedly assumed control of Twitter as its new owner. The Associated Press reported and confirmed, based on statements provided by the police, that the assailant used a hammer to break into the Pelosi residence and smash his way through a rear door just before 2:30 a.m. on Friday (Ortutay, 2022). The Santa Monica Observer's website published an article the day after the attack with the headline "The Awful Truth: Early on Friday morning, Paul Pelosi was drunk once more and engaged in a dispute with a male prostitute" (Greene, 2022). Without supporting evidence, the narrative asserted that Pelosi had invited DePape to his home after meeting him at a gay bar. Again without evidence, the article, which purported to describe "what happened," claimed that DePape was Pelosi's "male prostitute." The Associated Press reported that DePape was a "two-decade resident of the San Francisco Bay Area" who "was known locally as a pro-nudity activist who had picketed naked at protests against laws requiring people to be clothed in public." The story also claimed that DePape was a "former Castro nudist protester." (Mascaro & Dazio, 2022). After witnessing the proliferation of the salacious elements of the fake story, it is essential to investigate how deliberate manipulation contributed to the rapid spread of the false narrative? Social media is the perfect platform for persuading hate speech because of how quickly rumours and false information can be transmitted and how challenging it is to refute them once they are. According to research, false narratives like the Pelosi fake news are spread online by appealing to people's emotions and feelings and swaying political opinions in a corrupt manner.

The research uses two credible new articles: "Intruder Wanted to Break Speaker Pelosi's Kneecaps, Federal Complaint Says" by Glenn Thrush of The New York Times and "Suspect in Paul Pelosi attack awoke him by standing over his bedside, documents show" by Paul LeBlanc of CBS. They are both fact-checking news organizations that are in agreement regarding their honesty. The research also uses two unreliable articles: "The Awful Truth: Paul Pelosi Was Drunk Again And In a Dispute With a Male Prostitute Early Friday Morning" by Stan Greene of The Santa Monica Observer and "Musk in Control of Twitter, Ousts Top Executives" by Evan Sernoffsky from the Oakland-based fox affiliate KTVU. Because it tends to be more novel than actual news, these stories show how false information spreads quicker, farther, more profound, and more widely than true information. The articles discuss how novel and unexpected information is more likely to be distributed, favouring falsity over truth. "Discursive Deflection: Accusation of 'Fake News and the Spread of Misand Disinformation in the Tweets of President Trump'" by Ross & Rivers is the first scholarly article used in the research. Another source is the scholarly article "The Future of Free Speech, Trolls, Anonymity, and Fake News Online" by Rainie & Albright. It addresses growing concerns that online interactions' content, tone, and intent are now influencing the public for economic and political gain through disinformation.

A propagandist, fanatic, or fraudster will make up a piece of information or a story and present it as reality on a program, a social media post, or an editorial source. The original poster's followers then share the information on other channels through reposting, retweeting, or some other method. Since the 2016 U.S. presidential election, the deliberate spread of online misinformation, particularly on social media platforms like Twitter and Facebook, has sparked extraordinary interest across several disciplines, according to the academic article "A Survey of Fake News: Fundamental Theories, Detection Methods, and Opportunities" (Zhou, & Zafarani, 2020). This focus is mainly the result of a more profound fear that the dominance of "fake news" has exacerbated political polarization, eroded public confidence in government, and threatened democracy. Recent studies that attempted to gauge the frequency of fake news on social media discovered that it is uncommon compared to other news items and is most commonly found among older, politically conservative Americans. Despite these results, many academics and other observers argue that purposefully created misinformation spread via social media is pervasive enough to qualify as an urgent problem. Since The Observer is "notorious for fake news," Elon Musk received criticism for tweeting the outlandish conspiracy theory about the assault on Paul Pelosi. (CBS, 2022). The same journal, The Observer, made the erroneous claim that Hillary Clinton "died on September 11 and was replaced by a body double" (Rugaber, 2022). Musk has not explained why he deleted his post or linked to the article in response to a tweet from Hillary Clinton denouncing the attack (CBS, 2022). When The Associated Press asked questions, Twitter did not react right away. Professor of public policy at Georgetown University Dan Moynihan stated in response to Musk's tweet in a New York Times article, "A big problem in contemporary American politics is that one party has become obsessed with conspiracy theories, encouraging radical responses including anti-democratic actions and violence." (Thrush, 2022). Elon Musk, a businessman with more than 100 million Twitter followers, serves as an example of how misinformation spreads because people with strong political opinions do not believe they are responsible for hate speech resulting in political animosity. They try to ignore it and assert that spreading misleading information has nothing to do with their politics. In actuality, though, it incites party members to despise the opposition when a particular political party declares that they want to abolish gay rights and Musk tweets a story about Pelosi having a gay lover, which is strategic political media.

According to the academic study "Bad news: why we fall for fake news," when people share or engage with internet disinformation, they have effectively been encouraged to do so by its creators. Different persuasion methods are suggested by influential social information processing models (Brotherton, 2020). Sometimes people make snap judgments based on heuristics and surrounding clues. Sharing information on social media is more likely to be impulsive and quick than something individuals think about doing. For instance, there are signs that users unconsciously and automatically use Facebook's interactive features. It is necessary to use a peripheral persuasive route. Therefore, heuristics or contextual signals will be the main factors influencing people's decisions to share, like, and so forth. All politicians, far-right or otherwise, may benefit from regular social media use. However, just like in the Trump and Bolsonaro campaigns, the harmful use of social media gives anti-democratic political groupings an inherent edge over their rivals. However, misuse of social media platforms can corrode democracy more covertly. According to the academic article "The Future of Free Speech, Trolls, Anonymity and Fake News Online," authoritarians do not just succeed by spreading their message; they also succeed by taking advantage of situations in which people are either passively supportive of democratic institutions or actively hostile to them (Rainie & Albright, 2017). Far-right parties benefit at the expense of opposing parties by attempting to boost political apathy and undermine faith in enduring institutions. Another way that fake news disproportionately benefits far-right politicians is by assisting them in fomenting social tensions. Parties on the far right stigmatize minority groups like the LGBTQ community. Their primary focus is inciting fear and hatred toward these groups by portraying them as frightening and dangerous. However, using social media to harm out-groups reputations with the general public is considerably more challenging. Politicians that support democracy are significantly less likely to gain from this anti-liberal rhetoric. They will not gain support by inciting prejudice since their core supporters are likelier to hold fundamental democratic principles of equality and freedom. Anti-democratic forces are better off the more enraged people are.

In order to increase public trust and correct fake news without legitimizing it, the news industry must produce high-quality journalism. People must exercise critical thought and be aware of the sources. The most crucial step is to stop people from automatically sharing something that offends them or aligns with their values. Scammers use this inclination by making their stories fit their targets' worldviews to get past their defences. Additionally, never share a post on social media without validating its accuracy. This is especially valid if information originates from a reliable source. Accepting that other people occasionally make mistakes is necessary to propagate the truth. Find the sources. Finally, keep the story's agenda in mind at all times. Their writers' agendas give the stories colour. Most news companies are profit-driven, and their accuracy plays a role in building their brand and bottom line. However, many newsgroups cater their material to specific consumer segments, making it more likely that they would cover stories from a particular viewpoint. This may distort their accuracy and cause them to align more closely with the expectations of their intended audience. For example, political parties exist to elect their candidates and advance their agendas. Any news coming from a campaign or candidate is, therefore, more dubious because they are always meant to persuade. This does not inherently render them incorrect, but one should be on the lookout for emotional appeals, claims that catastrophe is imminent unless one takes action, or other strategies to instill dread or anxiety. Check for the facts before sharing
